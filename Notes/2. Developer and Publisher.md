# Developer and Publisher

## Game Components
- Source Code
    * Game Engine, Game Logic, Tools, ...
- Graphics Assets
    * 3D Models, Textures, Videos, Effects, ...
- Sound Assets
    * SFX, Music, Speech, Atmosphere, ...
- Game Design
    * Story, Characters, Level Design, Dialogues, ...

## Roles in Game Design

### Engineers (Programmers)
> Engineers design and implement the actual software.

- Runtime programmers 
    * Engine and the game itself
- Tool programmers
    * Dev tools
- Specialists
    * Rendering, AI, audio etc...

Some engineers are generalists.

### Artists
> Artists procude visual and audio content (assets).

- Concept artists
- 3D modelers
    * Foreground modelers
        - Characters, vehicles (objects that populate the game world)
    * Background modelers
        - Static background elements (buildings, terrain and bridges)
- Texture artist
- Lighting artist
- Animators
- Visual effects artists
- Technical artists
- UI artists
- Motion capture artists
- Voice actors
- Sound designers
- Music composers

### Game Designers
> Game Designers design the interactive elements of a game (the gameplay).
- Level designer, system designer, scripter, combat designer, creative director,
- Need strong communication skills
- Different levels of detail
    * **Macro level**
    Story arcs, sequences, goals / objectives 
    * **Individual levels**
    Level geometry (grey boxing), spawn areas, puzzles etc.
    * **Technical level**
    Scripts, AI, probabilities


### Quality Assurance (QA)
> Play the game continuously to find technical and design problems during development.
Quality assurance is crucial to the successful completion of a game.
- Testers need patience, persistence and great communication skills to be able to find and report problems.

### Producer
> Producers manage the schedule and human resources. They act as a bridge between the development team and the business unit.
**Timeline management**
- Milestone plan (external)
- Task plan (usually internal)

**Contract management**
- Development contracts
- Contact with external partners

### Support Staff
> Support staff don't work on the game directly, but exeucte tasks aournd the development process.
- Studio executive manager
- Marketing department
- Administrative staff
- IT department

## Developer Categories

### First Party Developers
> Game studios owned directly by a console manufacturer.
- May either use the name of the company itself, or have a specific division name.

### Second Party Developers
> Non-owned third-party developer that specializes in development for a particular platform.
- May have exclusive publishing agreements or other business relationships with a particular manufacturer while maintaining independence.
- May be wholly owned and funded by their parent company, but these are all considered subsidiaries.

### Third Party Developers
> Usually called upon by a publisher to develop a title for one or more systems. Usually publishers fund the studio on a per-game basis.

### Indie Developers
> Small software developers that are not owned by or beholden to a single publisher.

Digital distribution of inexpensive games makes it
possible to get wide distribution for their games.

# Video Game Development Team Sizes

## Small Teams
> Up to 20 people.

Usually split by major responsibilities:
- Designers
- Programmers
- Artists
- Project Manager / Producer

Developers are typically **generalists** and often work across multiple disciplines depending on the project's needs. Communication is usually informal, and everyone has a broad understanding of the project.

### Typical Structure
- 1 - 3 Designers
- 3 - 8 Programmers
- 2 - 8 Artists
- 0 - 2 Audio specialists (often outsourced)
- 1 Producer / Project Manager

### Advantages
- Fast communication and decision-making
- High flexibility and adaptability
- Little management overhead
- Team members have broad knowledge of the project
- Easier to maintain a unified vision

### Disadvantages
- Individuals have many responsibilities
- Limited specialization
- Knowledge loss if a team member leaves
- Difficult to develop very large or content-heavy games
- Limited resources and budget



## Medium Teams
> Approximately 20 - 70 people.

As projects become larger, the team becomes more specialized. Departments become more clearly separated, and coordination becomes increasingly important.

Team leads often coordinate each discipline.

### Advantages
- Greater specialization and expertise
- Can develop larger and more polished games
- Better distribution of workload
- More parallel development

### Disadvantages
- Increased communication overhead
- More coordination and planning required
- Decisions take longer
- Higher development costs
- Greater dependency between teams

## Large Teams
> More than 70 people.

Used for AAA games with large budgets and long development cycles. Teams are highly specialized and often distributed across multiple studios or countries.

### Typical Structure
Each department usually has:
- Team Lead
- Senior Developers
- Mid-Level Developers
- Junior Developers
Several producers and project managers coordinate the work across departments.

### Advantages
- High degree of specialization
- Able to build very large and technically complex games
- Large amounts of content can be produced in parallel
- Better scalability
- Experts available for nearly every discipline

### Disadvantages
- Very high communication overhead
- Expensive to manage
- Increased bureaucracy
- Slower decision-making
- Integration between teams becomes difficult
- Risk of departments becoming isolated ("silos")
- Individual developers often have little influence on the overall project

# Comparison

| Team Size | Typical Size | Organization | Main Strength | Main Weakness |
|------------|-------------:|--------------|---------------|---------------|
| Small | Up to 20 | Mostly generalists | Fast, flexible, low overhead | Limited manpower and specialization |
| Medium | 20 - 70 | Mixed generalists and specialists | Good balance between flexibility and expertise | More coordination required |
| Large | 70+ | Highly specialized departments | Can develop AAA-scale games | High communication and management overhead |

## Publisher relations
> Publishers usually are responsible for the product's manufacturing and marketing, including market research and advertising.
They usually **finance the development**.

Publishers usually try to manage development risk with a staff of producers or project
managers to monitor the progress of the developer, critique ongoing development, and
assist as necessary.

### Development contracts
Contracts specify a list of milestones intended to be delivered.
The publisher can give direction to the developer if the game is turning out other than as expected in some way.
When each milestone is completed and accepted, the publisher pays the developer an advance on royalties.

#### What if a milestone's acceptance criteria is not met by 100%?

The publisher will usually review the milestone against the agreed acceptance criteria.

Possible outcomes:
- The milestone is accepted despite minor issues.
- The developer must fix the issues before acceptance.
- The publisher may reject the milestone until the requirements are fulfilled.
- Payment is often delayed until the milestone is accepted, or payouts are only given partially.

---

#### What if the developer doesn't deliver a milestone in time?
Most contracts define deadlines for each milestone.

Possible consequences:
- The developer must provide a recovery plan.
- The publisher may grant an extension.
- Contractual penalties may apply.
- Future milestone payments may be delayed.
- In severe cases, the publisher may terminate the contract.

---

#### What if the publisher refuses or misses to pay?

The developer can usually:
- Issue a payment reminder.
- Charge interest for late payment (if defined by the contract or applicable law).
- Suspend further development.
- Ultimately terminate the contract and pursue legal action.

---

#### What if the publisher cancels the game in the middle of production?

This depends on the contract.

Typical arrangements include:
- The developer is paid for completed milestones.
- The contract specifies termination compensation.
- Work stops immediately.
- Ownership of unfinished work depends on the agreed intellectual property clauses.

---

#### What about deliverables from the publisher?
The publisher also has obligations, for example:
- Providing game requirements.
- Delivering assets, licenses or middleware.
- Providing feedback and milestone acceptance.
- Supplying localization or marketing materials.
- Making milestone payments on time.

Delays by the publisher may also delay the project schedule.

---

#### What about contractual penalties?
Contracts may define penalties for breaches such as:
- Missing deadlines.
- Failing quality requirements.
- Breaking confidentiality.
- Violating intellectual property rights.

The contract normally specifies how penalties are calculated and when they apply.

---

#### What if the publisher makes billions of dollars selling the game?

It depends on the payment model.

Possible arrangements:
- **Work-for-hire** 
The developer only receives the agreed contract payments.
- **Royalty agreement** 
The developer additionally receives a percentage of the game's revenue or profit.
- **Revenue sharing** 
Both parties share income according to the contract.
- **Bonus targets** 
The contract may specify performance goals, which award bonusses when hit (Game scores, sale targets).

---

#### Who is in trouble if the game turns out to be a financial flop?
The financial risk depends on the contract.

Generally:
- The publisher bears the commercial risk because they finance, market and sell the game.
- The developer still receives the agreed milestone payments if contractual obligations were fulfilled.
- If the developer also invested money or agreed to revenue sharing, they share part of the financial risk.

---

#### Can one developer have multiple of these contracts at once?
Yes, but it depends on their studio (e.g. third vs second party).
Additionally, contracts may contain exclusivity clauses that restrict this even for third party developers..

---

#### What role do in-game purchases play for the developer?
This depends entirely on the contract.

Possible situations:
- The publisher receives all revenue from microtransactions.
- The developer receives royalties on all game revenue, including in-game purchases.
- The developer receives bonuses if revenue targets are reached.

Most work-for-hire contracts do not include additional income from microtransactions.

---

#### Who owns the utilization rights?
This is defined by the intellectual property (IP) section of the contract.

Common models:
- The publisher receives exclusive utilization rights.
- The publisher owns the complete IP (most common for AAA games).
- The developer retains ownership and grants the publisher a license.
- Rights may transfer only after full payment.

---

#### What happens if either the publisher or developer becomes bankrupt?

##### If the developer becomes bankrupt
- Development may stop.
- The publisher may terminate the contract.
- Rights to unfinished work depend on the contract and bankruptcy law.
- Another studio may continue development.

##### If the publisher becomes bankrupt
- Milestone payments may stop.
- The developer becomes one of the publisher's creditors.
- Rights to the game become part of the bankruptcy proceedings.
- Another publisher may purchase the project and continue development.

#### Recoupment
Publishers usually retain 100% (or a large share) of a game's profits up until they have recouped their up front investment (*break even*) to guarantee a **Return on Investment**.
Afterwards, profitsharing usually proceeds with a defined revenue-split.

## Self Publishing
### Benefits
- No revenue splitting
- No transfer of intellectual property
- Flexible deadlines and release schedules
- 100% creative freedom
### Drawbacks
- 100% of the financial risk
- Marketing must be handled
- Business contracts must be handled
- Porting to multiple platforms is very difficult and time consuming
- You may not be able to develop for consoles
- Management and coordination require a lot of experience and expertise
- No outside input - you may end up developing in your own bubble

## External Service Providers
A Service Provider (SP) is a company that provides organizations with consulting,
legal, real estate, education, communications, storage, processing, and many other
services.

Developers and publishers outsource tasks to specialized SPs
- Relief of internal resources
    * Asset creation, programming, writing, design, testing, etc.
- Things that can't be handled internally
    * Motion capturing, voice recording, orchestration, media production etc.
- Things that require very special skills
    * Translations, legal advise, knowledge about certain target groups, etc.
