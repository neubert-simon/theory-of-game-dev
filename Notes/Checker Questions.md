## Describe how the Free-To-Play (F2P) business model generally works and what methods it uses to generate revenue.
Playing the game itself comes with no upfront cost. Games can employ a variety of ways to monetize in-game content: 
- **Microtransactions**
The player can buy in-game items or cosmetics via real world currency (or by buying in-game currency first).
- **Season Pass**
A season pass can be on offer, which award the player additional seasonal content and items.
Usually, the content can be played for free (at least in part), but bonuses and exlusive items are locked behind the paywall.
- **Advertising**
There might be adverts in the game, especially in use in mobile games.
- **Freemium model**
Some games make the game hardly playable if the player does not pay, encouraging the purchase of in-game items ect.


## There are four different kinds of video game development companies. Give a short explanation for each of the four kinds.

### (i) First-party
A development studio owned by a platform owner, developing games exclusively for that platform.

### (ii) Second-party
A generally independent development studio, which is in an exclusive relationship with a platform owner, specializing in developing for their platform. If their contractual relationship were to end, they would generally be able to develop for any platform.

### (iii) Third-party
Independent development studios, usually approached by publishers to develop games for one or more target platforms. 

### (iv) Independent
Usually mall game studios, unbeholden to publishers.

## Development contracts between a developer and a publisher are usually based on milestones. Briefly explain four typical features of such contracts and their significance for the developer.
- Milestone deadlines
Each milestone has a deadline. If met, the developers may receive payouts for each milestone, further funding development. If missed, extensions might need to be negotiated and financial penalties could be hitting the payouts.
- Milestone acceptance criteria
Each milestone usually has acceptance criteria. If met, the payouts may happen. If they are not met, work may have to be improved, and financial penalties might kick in as well.
- Intellectual property agreements
Developers usually agree to give up the intellectual property to the publisher for the work they have done.
- Profit splits
Publishers usually retain any profits (or a large percentage) until their up-front investment has been recouped, after which a profit split more favorable to the development studios kicks in.
