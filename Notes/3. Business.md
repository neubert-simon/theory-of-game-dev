# Games Business

## $ MONEY $
- Games need to be financed in advance
- Game design and money are inter-dependent
- Financial decisions affect the game design

> Since video games are products, and products need to make profit, developers must keep the target audience and market in mind.

The target audience pays for the game, therefore the game should satisfy their demands and be marketable to a broad enough consumer base.

## Business Models
### Retail
<img src="img/3. Business/retail-cash-flow.png" width=500>

Retailers receive copies from the publisher, with the option to send them back if they don't sell.

### Digital Distribution
> **Premium Games** are games purchased via one-time fee for the complete product.

### Downloadable Content
> **Expansions** are smaller chunks of content which get additionally sold on top of the base product.

### Pre-Order Bonuses
> Bonuses for pre-orders, which help raise funds shortly before release.

### Subscription Models
> **Recurring** payments for game content.

- Pay To Play
The whole game is locked behind the subscription.
- Pay for Premium
Subscriptions provide extra content / items / levels etc.

#### Season Pass
> Season specific activities, content, and items locked behind a paywall.

Content can usually be played for free, but the social and bonus aspects are paywalled.

### Free To Play
> Games that don't charge the player to play.

Running costs:
- Continuous Development
- Server Costs

Support themselves through microtransactions, season passes, or other monetization schemes.

#### Fremium Games
> Pay to Win games, might even degenerate into Pay To Play. 

Soft caps may be placed on players, making the game a grind or basically unplayable without paying up.

### Microtransactions
> Small, inexpensive transactions providing in-game items or cosmetics.

#### Loot boxes
> Chance based microtransactions (*gambling*).

### Early Access
- Reduces playtesting costs
- Funds development early (possibly without a publisher) 
➡️ Usually used by Indie Games

### Crowdfunding
> Promising a game and deliverables based on raised amounts. The audience acts as the publisher in the sense of providing the financing.

## Monetization Tricks

### Coercive Monetization
> Trick a person into making a purchase with incomplete information.
- Applying additional stress → short term “pain relief” by purchasing

###  Premium Currencies
> Intermediate currency ofuscates real value.
- Used to skirt regulation because you aren’t using real currency.
- Amounts are never enough to buy just what you want
- Better value for larger purchases

###  Skill Games vs. Money Games
> Disguise your money game as a skill game.

The game's difficulty ramps up massively, shifting it from a skill game to a money game.

###  Reward Removal
> Giving the player some huge reward, and then threatening to take it away if they do not spend money.

###  Progress Gates
> Players need to spend money if they want to go further in the game.
- Progress gates can be “hard” or “soft”
    * A "hard" gate is one where you cannot advance if you do not pay up.
    * A "soft" gate is one where the player can get past the gate, eventually.

###  Soft and Hard Boosts
- "Soft" boosts have an instant one-time effect.
- "Hard" boosts stick around either forever or until they are converted to something else.

### Ante Games (aka “Pay to Win”)
> These games start off with the appearance of a skill game. At some point players keep raising their antes, hoping that the others will fold.

###  Loot Boxes
> Endless spending opportunity with no guaranteed outcome.
- Exploits peoples' gambling habits.
